INVESTOR BEHAVIOR

Do investors play by
a different rulebook?

Investors are often assumed to have an advantage. We compare investor and non-investor transactions to see where that advantage appears in the data — and where the story is more complicated.

What does the data show? How to read this. The clearest difference is repeat participation: investors are much more likely to appear multiple times in the transaction data. But not every metric points the same way — investors do not always flip more often, and flip timelines are similar across buyer types. Price increases tend to be higher for investor-linked transactions, but those differences may reflect timing, property type, renovation strategy, and holding period. In short: investors do not always move faster — but they appear more repeatedly, giving them more exposure to Boston's rising market.

THE COST OF TIME

You'll really pay for The bank prices a 30-year mortgage at the monthly payment below. But that math assumes a clear runway — no kids, no student loans, no single-income years, no parents to support. As each of those land on you, the only way to keep the monthly payment manageable is to refinance into longer terms. That's why your real payoff age lands past 52 — and why the number below is longer than 30.
